ABSTRACT

This study focuses on the assembly and testing of Simple Aurora Monitors (SAM) in environments with significant magnetic noise. The primary objective is to measure Earth's magnetic field and distinguish natural variations from noise generated by human-made technology.

INTRODUCTION

The aurora borealis, or northern lights, is a natural light display predominantly seen in high-latitude regions. It is caused by the collision of solar wind and magnetospheric charged particles with the high altitude atmosphere. The measurement of the Earth's magnetic field helps in understanding these phenomena.

METHODS

Participants

The SAM instruments were assembled with magnetometer sensors. The testing involved placing these instruments in various environments with known magnetic noise sources, such as urban areas with heavy electronic usage, to evaluate their performance in isolating natural magnetic field variations.

RESULTS

The SAM instruments successfully detected natural magnetic field variations and distinguished them from noise in most test environments. However, in areas with extremely high levels of electronic noise, some data contamination was observed.

CONCLUSION

The study demonstrates the efficacy of SAM instruments in measuring Earth's magnetic field in noisy environments. Future work will focus on improving noise filtering techniques to enhance data accuracy in extremely noisy areas.
